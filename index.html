An Open Letter to the FBI

The Data Gap

The FBI's crime statistics systematically obscure Hispanic involvement in violent crime, inflate the "White" crime rate, and produce national data that is structurally misleading. Here's the evidence — and what needs to change.

Addressed to the CJIS Division  ·  The CJIS Advisory Policy Board  ·  The Director of the FBI
Scroll

Section I

The system is broken by design

The FBI collects race and ethnicity as two separate data fields from two different pools of agencies. When a Hispanic individual is arrested, they are coded as "White" in the race field and "Hispanic" in the ethnicity field. When the FBI publishes the data, these two fields cannot be cross-referenced.

Under the legacy "Crime in the United States" publications (through 2019), the most widely cited table was Table 43A — "Arrests by Race and Ethnicity." Its own footnote admitted the problem:

"The ethnicity totals are representative of those agencies that provided ethnicity breakdowns. Not all agencies provide ethnicity data; therefore, the race and ethnicity totals will not equal." — FBI Table 43A, Footnote 2

In plain language: the race column and the ethnicity column come from different sets of police departments. You cannot subtract one from the other. The math doesn't work because the denominators are different.

The instinct is to subtract: 3,650 − 1,341 = 2,309 non-Hispanic White murder arrests. This calculation is invalid. The 3,650 and the 1,341 represent overlapping but non-identical groups of agencies. The actual number of non-Hispanic White murder arrests in the United States in 2019 is unknowable from published FBI data.

Section II

The Crime Data Explorer made it worse

When the FBI retired the numbered table format and launched the Crime Data Explorer as its primary public interface, the underlying data problem was not fixed — it was buried deeper.

The CDE presents race and ethnicity on separate tabs. A user researching homicide offenders clicks the "Race" tab and sees one set of numbers. To find Hispanic data, they must separately click the "Ethnicity" tab — with no indication that the two datasets may come from different agency pools or that the "White" figure on the Race tab includes Hispanic individuals.

Section V

The federal government already mandated this change

On March 28, 2024, the Office of Management and Budget revised Statistical Policy Directive No. 15 — the first major update since 1997. The new standard requires all federal agencies to adopt a combined race/ethnicity question by September 2029.

The OMB made this change because its own research confirmed the two-question format was failing. In the 2020 Census, over 43% of Hispanic respondents either did not answer the race question or selected "Some Other Race." The SOR population surged 129% between 2010 and 2020, surpassing the Black/African American population — driven almost entirely by Hispanic respondents who could not find themselves in the categories offered.

The revised minimum reporting categories are: American Indian or Alaska Native, Asian, Black or African American, Hispanic or Latino, Middle Eastern or North African, Native Hawaiian or Pacific Islander, and White.

The FBI, as a federal agency, is bound by SPD 15. The question is not whether the FBI will adopt combined reporting, but when — and whether it will act with urgency or wait until the deadline while continuing to publish misleading data.

→ OMB Revised SPD 15, Federal Register (March 29, 2024)

Section VI

The cost of inaction

Every year the FBI publishes crime data under the old framework, the following harms compound.

R

Researchers cannot isolate Hispanic crime rates at the national level. Studies using the subtraction method introduce systematic error. Decades of peer-reviewed criminological literature are built on data that conflates two distinct populations.

J

Journalists reporting on crime operate with data that makes it impossible to accurately describe who is committing and experiencing violence. This fuels both underreaction and overreaction — because the numbers are wrong.

P

Policymakers deciding law enforcement funding, violence intervention programs, and community investment are working with data that cannot tell them which communities are most affected. Resources flow to the wrong places.

C

Communities are harmed on both sides. Hispanic communities experiencing disproportionate violence are statistically invisible. Non-Hispanic White communities are statistically burdened with crime rates that are not theirs. Neither is served.

Section VII

What we are asking

  1. Publish an implementation timeline for adopting OMB's revised SPD 15 standards in the UCR and NIBRS, consistent with the September 2029 federal deadline.

  2. Prioritize the combined race/ethnicity question in NIBRS data collection, ensuring that participating agencies code Hispanic/Latino as a co-equal racial/ethnic category.

  3. Publish bridging tables that allow researchers to compare historical data collected under the old framework with data collected under the new combined framework.

  4. Immediately begin publishing supplemental tables that present violent crime data with Hispanic/Latino as a separate category — even before full implementation — using agencies that already report complete ethnicity data.

  5. Consult with state-level agencies that have already adopted combined reporting — including the California Department of Justice and the New York City Police Department — to draw on decades of implementation experience.

  6. Disclose the limitation on the Race tab itself within the Crime Data Explorer interface. Users should see — without navigating to a separate page — that "White" includes Hispanic individuals and that race and ethnicity data may represent different agency pools.

The FBI's crime data is the most cited source on crime in the United States. Right now, it is systematically wrong about the nation's largest ethnic minority.

The OMB has determined the two-question framework is broken. The largest state and the largest city already report with Hispanic separated. The federal mandate is in place. The path is clear.

What remains is the will to act — and the urgency to act now, not in 2029.
