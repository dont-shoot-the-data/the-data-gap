# OPEN LETTER TO THE FBI CRIMINAL JUSTICE INFORMATION SERVICES DIVISION

## RE: Urgent Need to Adopt Combined Race/Ethnicity Reporting in the Uniform Crime Report and National Incident-Based Reporting System

---

**To:**
The Honorable Director, Federal Bureau of Investigation
The CJIS Advisory Policy Board
The Criminal Justice Information Services Division

**From:**
Concerned Researchers, Criminologists, Data Analysts, and Public Policy Advocates

**Date:**
March 2026

---

## I. Summary

We are writing to urge the FBI's Criminal Justice Information Services (CJIS) Division to adopt a combined race/ethnicity classification system in the Uniform Crime Report (UCR) and National Incident-Based Reporting System (NIBRS) — one that treats Hispanic or Latino as a co-equal racial/ethnic category alongside White, Black, Asian, and other groups, rather than coding it as a separate ethnicity field.

This is not a novel request. The Office of Management and Budget mandated exactly this change in its March 28, 2024 revision of Statistical Policy Directive No. 15 (SPD 15), requiring all federal agencies to adopt a combined race/ethnicity question by September 2029. The California Department of Justice has reported crime data with Hispanic as a distinct category for decades. The New York City Police Department does the same. Both jurisdictions produce data that is dramatically more transparent, more useful, and more honest than what the FBI currently publishes.

The FBI's continued use of a two-category system — where "Hispanic" is an ethnicity layered on top of a separate race classification — produces national crime statistics that systematically obscure Hispanic involvement in violent crime, artificially inflate the "White" crime rate, and render it impossible for researchers, journalists, policymakers, or the public to understand who is committing and experiencing violence in the United States.

This is a data quality problem with real-world consequences.

---

## II. The Problem: How the Current System Fails

### A. The Two-Category Framework

The FBI's current crime reporting framework follows the pre-2024 OMB standard: race (White, Black, American Indian/Alaska Native, Asian, Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander) and ethnicity (Hispanic or Latino / Not Hispanic or Latino) are collected as separate data elements. In practice, this means that a Hispanic individual arrested for a violent crime is typically coded as **"White"** in the race field and **"Hispanic"** in the ethnicity field.

This problem has persisted across two generations of FBI reporting systems. Under the legacy "Crime in the United States" publications (through 2019), the most widely cited source was Table 43A ("Arrests by Race and Ethnicity"), which presented race and ethnicity in side-by-side columns drawn from **different pools of reporting agencies**. Table 43A's own footnote stated:

> "The ethnicity totals are representative of those agencies that provided ethnicity breakdowns. Not all agencies provide ethnicity data; therefore, the race and ethnicity totals will not equal."

This meant the two columns could not be cross-referenced. A researcher looking at the 2019 data saw 3,650 "White" murder arrests in the race column and 1,341 "Hispanic" murder arrests in the ethnicity column — but subtracting one from the other to estimate non-Hispanic White arrests (3,650 − 1,341 = 2,309) was mathematically invalid. The 3,650 and the 1,341 represented overlapping but non-identical sets of agencies. The actual number of non-Hispanic White murder arrests in 2019 was **unknowable** from published FBI data.

### B. The Crime Data Explorer Makes It Worse

When the FBI retired the numbered table format and launched the Crime Data Explorer (CDE) as its primary public interface (https://cde.ucr.cjis.gov/LATEST/webapp/#/pages/explorer/crime/crime-trend), the underlying data problem was not fixed — it was obscured further.

The CDE presents race and ethnicity data on **separate tabs** within its query interface. A user researching homicide offenders in a given jurisdiction must click the "Race" tab to see one set of numbers, then separately click the "Ethnicity" tab to see another — with no indication on either tab that the two datasets may represent different agency pools or that the "White" figure on the Race tab includes an unknown number of Hispanic individuals.

Under the old Table 43A format, at least the two columns appeared on the same page and the footnote warning was immediately visible. The CDE's tabbed interface eliminates even that minimal transparency. A journalist, policymaker, or member of the public can now query the FBI's official crime database, see "White" listed as the largest offender category on the Race tab, and never realize that a separate Ethnicity tab exists — let alone that the "White" figure they are reading is inflated by the inclusion of Hispanic offenders who cannot be disaggregated from the total.

### C. The Ethnicity Data Gap

The scale of missing ethnicity data compounds this problem. In 2019, the race column of Table 43A reported 7,964 total murder arrests. The ethnicity column reported only 6,474 — a gap of 1,490 arrests, or 18.7% of the total. Nearly one in five murder arrests had **no ethnicity data at all**. For other crime categories, the gap is often larger.

This means the FBI publishes a national "White" arrest figure that includes an unknown number of Hispanic individuals, then publishes a separate "Hispanic" figure from a smaller subset of agencies, and offers no way to reconcile the two. The result is a set of national crime statistics in which:

- The **"White" crime rate is inflated** by the inclusion of Hispanic offenders
- The **Hispanic crime rate is invisible** at the national level
- The **non-Hispanic White crime rate cannot be calculated**
- Researchers who attempt the subtraction method produce **invalid estimates** that appear authoritative

## III. The Evidence: What Happens When You Separate the Data

The strongest argument for changing the FBI's system is empirical. Multiple major jurisdictions already report crime data with Hispanic as a distinct racial/ethnic category. When they do, the picture changes fundamentally.

### A. New York City (NYPD)

The New York City Police Department publishes annual enforcement reports that classify victims, suspects, and arrestees using five categories: American Indian, Asian/Pacific Islander, Black, White, and Hispanic. The NYPD explicitly notes that "Black and White used in tables and charts throughout this report therefore represent Black Non-Hispanic and White Non-Hispanic."

For Murder and Non-Negligent Manslaughter in 2020, the NYPD reported the following distribution of known-race arrestees:

| Race/Ethnicity | Victims | Suspects | Arrestees |
|----------------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Black | 65.0% | 63.4% | 60.2% |
| Hispanic | 26.4% | 26.8% | 32.1% |
| White | 6.1% | 6.3% | 3.6% |
| Asian/Pac. Isl. | 2.5% | 3.5% | 4.1% |

The critical figure: **White (non-Hispanic) individuals accounted for just 3.6% of murder arrestees in New York City.** Under the FBI's system, many of those Hispanic arrestees — 32.1% of the total — would be coded as "White" in the race field, inflating the White share by nearly tenfold.

### B. California (Department of Justice)

The California Department of Justice publishes annual "Crime in California" reports using four race/ethnic categories: White, Hispanic, Black, and Other. California's Table 31 (Felony Arrests by Race/Ethnic Group, 2024) reports the following for homicide:

| Race/Ethnicity | Number | Percent |
|----------------|--------|---------|
| White | 212 | 16.2% |
| Hispanic | 629 | 48.2% |
| Black | 389 | 29.8% |
| Other | 75 | 5.7% |

Again, the pattern is stark. Hispanic individuals account for nearly half of all homicide arrests in California. Non-Hispanic White individuals account for 16.2%. Under the FBI's system, California's "White" figure would absorb the entire Hispanic total, producing a combined "White" figure of approximately **64.4%** — a number that bears no resemblance to the reality on the ground and would wildly misrepresent the racial/ethnic composition of homicide offending in the state.

### C. The National Distortion

The implications for national data are severe. If the pattern observed in New York City and California holds across major urban jurisdictions — and there is strong reason to believe it does — then the FBI's published "White" arrest figures for violent crime are substantially inflated by the inclusion of Hispanic offenders who cannot be disaggregated from the total.

This is not a minor technical footnote. This is a structural flaw that shapes how America understands violence, how resources are allocated, and how policy is made.

## IV. The OMB Has Already Mandated This Change

On March 28, 2024, the Office of Management and Budget published revisions to Statistical Policy Directive No. 15 — the first major update since 1997. The revised standard requires all federal agencies to:

1. **Use a combined race/ethnicity question** rather than separate race and ethnicity fields
2. **Treat Hispanic or Latino as a co-equal category** alongside White, Black, Asian, American Indian/Alaska Native, Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander, and the new Middle Eastern/North African category
3. **Allow respondents to select multiple categories** to reflect multiracial/multiethnic identity
4. **Implement these changes by September 2029**

The OMB made this change because its own research — and the 2020 Census results — demonstrated that the two-question format was failing. Over 43% of Hispanic respondents either did not answer the race question or selected "Some Other Race," making the separate-question approach functionally broken for the nation's largest ethnic minority. The "Some Other Race" population grew 129% between 2010 and 2020, surpassing the Black/African American population to become the second-largest race group — driven almost entirely by Hispanic respondents who could not find themselves in the race categories offered.

The FBI, as a federal agency, is bound by SPD 15. The question is not *whether* the FBI will adopt combined race/ethnicity reporting, but *when* — and whether the agency will act with the urgency this problem demands, or wait until the compliance deadline while continuing to publish misleading data.

---

## V. The Cost of Inaction

Every year the FBI continues to publish crime data under the old framework, the following harms accumulate:

**For researchers:** Peer-reviewed criminological studies that rely on UCR/NIBRS data cannot isolate Hispanic crime rates at the national level. Studies that attempt the subtraction method introduce systematic error into the literature. Decades of published research are built on a foundation that conflates two distinct populations.

**For journalists:** Media reporting on crime — already a politically charged topic — operates with data that makes it impossible to accurately describe who is committing and experiencing violence. This fuels both underreaction and overreaction, because the underlying numbers are wrong.

**For policymakers:** Federal, state, and local officials making decisions about law enforcement funding, violence intervention programs, public health initiatives, and community investment are working with data that cannot tell them which communities are most affected. Resources flow to the wrong places.

**For the public:** Americans trying to understand crime in their communities are given national statistics that systematically misrepresent reality. This erodes trust in public data and creates space for misinformation to fill the gap.

**For affected communities:** Hispanic communities experiencing disproportionate violence are statistically invisible in national data. Non-Hispanic White communities are statistically burdened with crime rates that are not theirs. Neither population is served by this arrangement.

---

## VI. What We Are Asking

We respectfully request that the FBI's CJIS Division take the following actions:

1. **Publish an implementation timeline** for adopting OMB's revised SPD 15 standards in the UCR and NIBRS, consistent with the September 2029 federal deadline.

2. **Prioritize the combined race/ethnicity question** in NIBRS data collection, ensuring that participating agencies code Hispanic/Latino as a co-equal racial/ethnic category rather than a separate ethnicity field.

3. **Publish bridging tables** that allow researchers to compare historical data (collected under the old two-question framework) with data collected under the new combined framework, following the bridging guidance provided in the OMB's revised SPD 15.

4. **Immediately begin publishing supplemental tables** that present violent crime data with Hispanic/Latino as a separate category — even before full NIBRS implementation — using data from agencies that already report complete ethnicity information. This interim step would provide the public with more accurate data while the full transition is underway.

5. **Consult with state-level agencies** that have already adopted combined reporting — including the California Department of Justice and the New York City Police Department — to draw on their implementation experience and methodological expertise.

6. **Publicly acknowledge the limitations** of the Crime Data Explorer's race and ethnicity tabs — and of the legacy Table 43A — by including clear, prominent guidance (not a buried footnote or a separate documentation page) explaining that "White" includes Hispanic individuals and that race and ethnicity data may represent different agency pools and cannot be cross-referenced. This disclosure should appear on the Race tab itself within the CDE interface, not require users to navigate to a separate methodology page.

---

## VII. Conclusion

The FBI's crime data is the most widely cited source on crime in the United States. It informs academic research, media coverage, legislative action, and public understanding. When that data systematically obscures the role of the nation's largest ethnic minority in crime — both as offenders and as victims — it fails everyone.

The OMB has already determined that the two-question race/ethnicity framework is broken. The largest state in the union and the largest city in the nation have already demonstrated that combined reporting works and produces more honest data. The path forward is clear, the precedent is established, and the federal mandate is in place.

What remains is the will to act — and the urgency to act now, not in 2029.
